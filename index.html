Hi! I research labour economics, or how people earn their livelihoods. I'm currently thinking about how new technologies affect the labour market, but I'm also interested in (among other things) statistics and machine learning, (political) philosophy, and atmospheric physics.

Previously, I researched persistent poverty at the New Zealand Treasury (Te Tai Ōhanga) using millions of tax records. Before that, I worked on labour income processes (how people's incomes vary over their lives) as part of John Geanakoplos and Stephen Zeldes' model of the US Social Security system.

I study Economics & Mathematics at Yale University.

Eigenvalues of neural nets have meaningful interpretations
project for Yale MATH 232, with Max Watzky, Yogev Angelovici, Ethan Martinez, and Juan Carlos Gonzalez.
We train a simple neural net to recognize handwritten digits using the MNIST dataset. We first compute singular values of the last layer weights. Then, we think of the neural net as a connected graph and compute eigenvalues of the graph Laplacian. By setting some of these eigenvalues to zero, we show that these eigenvalues correspond to recognizable features and influence the net's ability to distinguish specific pairs of digits.

Privacy with Asymmetrically Informed Consumers
project for CPSC 6551 (Foundations of Data Economics)
A classic model of dynamic pricing by Taylor shows that data disclosure by sellers is not necessarily good or bad for welfare. In particular, data disclosure may be good for welfare if low-willingness-to-pay consumers are charged less, or if sophisticated consumers can pretend to be low-willingness-to-pay types. I give a simplified exposition of this model, and I extend the model to when when consumers are uncertain about what their data will be used for. First, consumers will strategically mask their identity if they have the slightest suspicion their data is being sold. Second, if consumers sell data to a firm instead of buying a good, everyone will be forced to sell for free.
